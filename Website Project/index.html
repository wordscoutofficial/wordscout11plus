What's the best way to learn? Simplified.

The science of memory and study, explained clearly with no jargon.

By Patricia Andreev

Overview

There is no single perfect way to learn. What matters is how your brain takes in, stores, and retrieves information. These four science-backed strategies make the biggest difference.

01

Be active: retrieval practice

Test yourself and explain ideas out loud. Studies show this produces stronger long-term memory than re-reading - the act of pulling a memory out is what strengthens it.

Read more about retrieval practice
02

Give it time: spaced repetition

Short sessions spread across days work far better than one long session. First studied by Ebbinghaus in 1885, the research has held up ever since.

Read more about spaced repetition
03

Make connections: elaborative interrogation

Ask yourself "why does this make sense?" after each new fact. Linking new ideas to existing knowledge makes memories far stickier.

Read more about elaborative interrogation
04

Teach it out loud: the Protégé Effect

Explaining a topic to someone else forces you to organise your thinking and exposes every gap. Your brain does not check if anyone is listening.

Read more about the Protégé Effect

Quick reminders

  • Testing yourself beats re-reading every single time
  • Studying a little each day beats one big cramming session
  • Ask "why?" after every new fact you learn
  • Try explaining topics out loud - even to your pet

The aim: remember and use what you learn, not just long enough for the exam, but genuinely and durably.
